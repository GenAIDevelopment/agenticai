# Gravitational Force

## Introduction
Gravity is a fundamental interaction that causes mutual attraction between all things with mass or energy. In classical mechanics, it is described by Newton's Law of Universal Gravitation.

## Newton's Law of Universal Gravitation
Every point mass attracts every other point mass by a force acting along the line intersecting both points. The force is proportional to the product of their masses and inversely proportional to the square of the distance between their centers.

### Formula
$$F = G \frac{m_1 m_2}{r^2}$$

- **F**: Gravitational force between the two objects (N)
- **G**: Gravitational constant ($6.674 \times 10^{-11} \text{ N m}^2/\text{kg}^2$)
- **$m_1, m_2$**: Masses of the objects (kg)
- **r**: Distance between the centers of the masses (m)

## Key Concepts
1. **Inverse-Square Law**: The force decreases rapidly as the distance between objects increases. Doubling the distance reduces the force to one-fourth.
2. **Weight vs. Mass**: 
    - **Mass** is the amount of matter in an object (constant everywhere).
    - **Weight** is the force of gravity on an object ($W = mg$). It depends on the local gravitational field strength ($g$).
3. **Gravitational Field**: A region of space surrounding a body in which another body experiences a force of gravitational attraction. Near Earth's surface, $g \approx 9.81 \text{ m/s}^2$.

## Circular Orbits
For an object in a stable circular orbit, gravity provides the centripetal force:
$$F_g = F_c$$
$$G \frac{Mm}{r^2} = \frac{mv^2}{r}$$
Where $M$ is the mass of the central body and $v$ is the orbital velocity.
